Ten Songs: Why things actually matter

July 13, 2026 Methodology By Josh

Ten songs is a harsh constraint, but a necessary one. Depending on the year you're looking at, hitting that exact number was either easy or a brutal exercise in "kill your darlings." Some years I genuinely struggled to find ten tracks, while others there were far too many!

The mid-to-late 1960s were particularly difficult for me to narrow down. This was the music I listened to growing up. Furthermore, it's the specific era that a lot of the bands I subsequently listened to during the 1990s were referencing, sampling, and pulling inspiration from. Whittling a momentous year like 1966 down was just rude. anyway!

But yeah, without the limit, an archive just becomes a playlist. It becomes passive. Forcing a ten-song cap means you have to make hard decisions about the narrative of that specific year. I have also tried to mold the 1000 songs as a narrative as a whole, so adding blue boy in 1997 looks back to Marlena Shaw's "Woman of the Ghetto" and that's on purpose!

10 Songs a Year

So yeah, I am trying to create an interesting, cohesive audio story spanning exactly 100 years, from 1930 to 2030. With heaters. That are a bit catchy.

  • Resonance: Does it actually mean something. Does it bring to mind physical spaces, late-night radios, and specific human context over simple passive background listening?
  • Me: Is the catalog a true reflection of personal, ear-crafted taste rather than an algorithmically optimized collection?

Anchor Years: Why 1996 is the Center of the Archive

July 17, 2026 Methodology By Josh
Featured Artists: White Town Lew Stone Bronski Beat

When building a 100-year archive, you need a place to stand. A vantage point. It came about by trying to truly reconnect with music, searching for historical connection points. This is why I often suggest people start their journey through the archive in 1996.

1996 was a pivotal year for me personally, but more importantly, it serves as the perfect starting point for the entire project. Standing in the mid-90s allows you to look deep into the past, while simultaneously casting an eye forward into the digital future. Well, a little forward, anyway. Then we stop. We stop at 2030 because this archive is not an open-ended projection of the future; it is about encapsulating a highly specific, relatively short era: 1930 to 2030.

The anchor year is 1996. Consider how Your Woman by White Town sounds so intrinsically of its era—a bedroom-produced electronic hit—yet it relies entirely on a muffled, crackling trumpet sample.

That sample is ripped straight from the 1932 track My Woman by Lew Stone (featuring Al Bowlly). By placing these two tracks in the same overarching archive, suddenly 1996 and 1932 are inextricably linked. The archive isn't a straight, boring chronological line; it's a web of influences, much like how Bronski Beat laid the groundwork for the synth-pop that would follow them.

This is why understanding the lineage matters. A hundred years is enough time to watch these cultural echoes bounce back and forth.
